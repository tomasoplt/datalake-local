Kladno – the industrial heart of BohemiaKladno is a city located approximately 25 kilometers northwest of Prague in the Central Bohemian Region. With approximately 68,000 inhabitants, it is one of the largest Czech cities and has historically been considered one of the most important industrial centers in Central Europe. The city was founded in the 13th century by Prince Slavibor II, who had a fortified castle built here to protect the trade routes leading from Prague to the west. The historical center of Kladno consists of the large Republic Square, around which stands the town hall from 1487, the Baroque church of St. Methodius and the Art Nouveau building of the Vichr theater built in 1912. In the 19th century, Kladno turned into an industrial center thanks to coal deposits and the construction of the Poldina Huť ironworks, named after the owner Leopold of Fürstenberg. The factory employed over 12,000 workers at the turn of the 19th and 20th centuries and produced steel for the entire Habsburg monarchy. Its remains today form the grounds of the Stará Huť industrial museum, which is visited by over 80,000 tourists annually. Kladno is also the birthplace of several important personalities. The writer and satirist Bohumil Pásek was born here, whose novels about the Kladno workers' environment are still read in secondary schools. The Kladno native, physicist Rudolf Novotný, also achieved world fame, and in 1961 he received the Nobel Prize for research into superconducting materials.
